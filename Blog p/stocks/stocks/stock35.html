Types of Orders

By Admin | Posted on 27 JULY 2024 | 07 min read

Limit Order

A limit order is a type of order used in trading financial instruments that specifies a price at which you are willing to buy or sell an asset. Unlike a market order, which executes immediately at the current market price, a limit order is only executed when the market price reaches your specified limit price.

Key Characteristics
  • Price Specification

    • Buy Limit Order: You set a maximum price you are willing to pay for an asset. The order is executed only if the market price is at or below this price.

    • Sell Limit Order: You set a minimum price at which you are willing to sell an asset. The order is executed only if the market price is at or above this price.

  • No Immediate Execution Guarantee

    • A limit order will only be executed if the market price meets your specified limit price. If the price does not reach your limit, the order may not be executed.

  • Control Over Execution Price

    • Limit orders provide control over the execution price, allowing you to set a precise price for buying or selling an asset.

Advantages of Limit Orders
  • Price Control

    • You can specify the exact price at which you want to buy or sell an asset, ensuring that you do not pay more or receive less than your desired price.

  • Reduced Slippage

    • Limit orders help minimize the impact of price slippage since they execute only at your specified price or better.

  • Strategic Trading

    • Useful for executing trades at specific price levels based on technical analysis or market forecasts.

Disadvantages of Limit Orders
  • No Execution Guarantee

    • There is no guarantee that your order will be executed if the market price does not reach your limit price. This can be problematic if the price moves away from your limit level.

  • Potential Delays

    • In volatile markets, your order might remain unfilled if the price does not reach your specified limit, which may delay your entry or exit.

  • Partial Fills

    • If there is insufficient liquidity, your limit order may be partially filled, where only a portion of the total order is executed.

Example in the Indian Market
Scenario

Suppose you want to buy shares of HDFC Bank, and the current market price is ₹1,600. You believe the price will drop, so you place a limit order to buy 100 shares at ₹1,550.

Execution

    Your limit buy order will only be executed if the price of HDFC Bank falls to ₹1,550 or below. If the price remains above ₹1,550, your order will not be filled, and it may expire if not canceled or adjusted.

Comparison

  • Market Order: Guarantees execution immediately at the current market price but does not guarantee the price.

  • Limit Order: Guarantees the price but does not guarantee execution, as the order will only be filled if the market reaches your specified limit price.

When to Use a Limit Order
  • When Price Precision is Important

    • Ideal for scenarios where you want to ensure that you buy or sell at a specific price or better, such as when entering or exiting positions based on technical analysis.

  • In Less Liquid Markets

    • Useful for trading assets with lower liquidity, where market prices may fluctuate significantly and slippage can be more pronounced.

  • For Strategic Entries/Exits

    • When you have a specific price target based on market conditions or analysis, limit orders allow you to execute trades at your preferred levels.

Conclusion

Market orders are designed for quick execution at the current market price, ensuring that trades are completed immediately. While they provide speed and simplicity, they come with the risk of price slippage and less control over the execution price, making them best suited for situations where immediate execution is critical, especially in highly liquid markets.
